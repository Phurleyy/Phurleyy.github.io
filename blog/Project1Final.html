How Cars Taught Me to Speak: An Automotive Ethnography

There I was, laying on the cold gravel with two jack stands to either side of me, my Lexus IS300’s 25 year old machinations looming above me. “What have I gotten myself into?” I thought to myself as I clicked the ratchet to the tighten setting and began trying to get the bolt back into the radiator support bracket. I remembered back to when I had first gotten into cars, and how far I’ve come now. I could barely change my own oil then; now I was changing a timing belt and water pump in the dead of winter? I called a friend of mine to come help, and realized something; I had finally found something that I could communicate to another person with, not with any strings attached or a lack of confidence, but as another individual, person to person.

Whew, working on this car sucked!

HUMBLE BEGINNINGS

 By the time I hit high school, I was probably the most awkward student out of my class. I felt estranged from them because of my lack of athleticism, my overreliance on online communication, and overall bad social and mental health (through no fault of my own, though. Covid hit during my middle school years and sort of messed up an incredible formative part of my life). I didn’t know how to read body language well if at all, how to break into conversation during those all-important group projects, and most importantly how to connect with my peers on a deeper than surface level. My grades started to suffer as the days ticked on. This wasn’t due to negligence, it was because I lacked friends to talk to, and even study with. Over time, I was slowly eroding. I knew something had to change, and fast.

When I turned 16, I woke up with a radiating smile, I was ready to finally get my license and car. I traveled to the DMV to get it, aced my driving test, and as quickly as I started my journey towards a license, it ended, and I had it. At this point my grades had recovered a little bit, and my social standing was a bit higher than it was when I was an underclassman, but still wasn’t anything to write home about. This all changed when I started looking for cars, though. I had a very small budget of around 3 thousand dollars for one (made from working odd summer jobs and a small part time position at Burlington Coat Factory). This helped constrain me on what I really needed in a car; reliability, practicality, and some semblance of fun. I was browsing ads on Facebook Marketplace for a while until I found it, my perfect first car– a 2000 Honda Civic Sedan. I went to pick it up with my mom in the dead of winter, the coldest day of the year… But my heart couldn’t have been less warm. It felt so freeing to finally be able to drive anywhere I wanted, do anything I wanted to do, and most importantly, on the cheap with my car’s little 1.6 liter gas sipper engine.


This is still the cleanest example of this model civic I have ever seen in person.

Soon the car started to show signs of wear and tear, an exhaust leak, then a misfire in the engine. I was a broke high school student with little money to my name, though, and couldn’t afford to take it to a shop where they would charge exorbitant labor fees. I knew what I had to do, I was just scared to do it. My dad ended up helping me work up the courage to get under my car and patch the exhaust leak I had with an exhaust band clamp kit– It was so satisfying knowing that I had the power to control at least this one thing in my life, how well my car ran.

This is a picture of me changing out my civic’s ignition system– I didn’t particularly know what I was doing, but it worked out in the end.

        I totaled the civic in a freak accident where my breaks went out (NOT a product of me working on the car) so I had to find a new car, and fast. Luckily, my aunt and uncle had a car for sale, one that I had never seen before or since: an IS300 sportcross. I did my research and learned that it was one of three thousand ever made across the world (rarer than most Ferrari and Lamborghini models). I snatched it up with all the savings I had remaining and began driving it. It deepened my appreciation for cars because it turned my automotive passion into a statement, something people could recognize and point me out from a crowd due to.


The police officers that came said if I was 6 feet closer to the railing I would’ve most likely died; talk about a close call.

DEEPER INTO CAR CULTURE

I soon began to do the oil changes of close friends and family, and people would come to me first and foremost with their automotive questions. It made me happy seeing that people could come and rely on me for such an important thing, keeping their mode of transportation reliable and roadworthy. Through these interactions, I further learned how to work on cars and it made me seem more trustworthy to other people. It felt like working harder for other people will make them want to work hard for me.

My IS300, in all of it’s scraping-the-ground-everywhere-I-go glory.

As I grew deeper into car culture, its positive effects started to grow deeper roots into me. I soon came into contact with local people who were into the same types of cars as I was, and started going to car shows with my humble civic sedan. People in the car community didn’t judge me for anything people at school judged me for; no hatred based on my perceived smartness (or lack thereof), how athletic I was, or how “popular” I was overall. All they cared about was how clean my car was and if I was taking good care of it or not. The language of love that they used was more of a fit for me than anything I have seen, before or after. I finally felt fluent in something, no matter how untraditional it may seem to an outsider.

PINNACLE OF ASPIRATIONS

Senior year, I had finally decided to commit entirely to my passion for cars– I bought a car that no normal person would purchase as a daily driver– a 25 year old, two door, gas guzzling V8 engined Camaro SS. Although this car wasn’t a logical choice, it helped me express who I am as a person in a language that I wanted to express myself in, which, in my opinion, matters more than anything else. I had lived so long without expressing myself in a language before I encountered car culture, with that outlet available to me, it’s like an entirely new world has opened up to me. I don’t know what I would do without this automotive passion of mine. It allowed me to express myself in ways I didn’t think possible, and I don’t think I’d be here at Notre Dame without it.

Once again, the cleanest example I’ve seen of a particular car is the one that is mine. Maybe I just like my cars extra nice?

It only comes to me now, as I sit here and write this post, how far I've come– not just in an automotive sense, but in a sense of personal development. I feel like I’ve taken a city of bricks and turned it into gold, as Julius Caesar said. When I started this automotive journey, I had little to my name– a crappy GPA, no motivation, no friends– and all of that has changed. Although I still do face some issues, they aren’t nearly as bad nowadays, and if I really feel crappy, I do the same thing every time… I go out for a drive.
